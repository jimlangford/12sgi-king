Lux et Veritas PONO

Light · Truth · Righteousness — the three pillars of civic education

Lux — Light — Transparency

Government data is public record. Our tools surface it — meeting agendas, vote rolls, contracts, donor lists — in plain language. Light means every student can see what is actually happening, not just what is reported.

Veritas — Truth — Primary Sources

The record leads. Our News vs Record system pairs every news story with the underlying primary source: the official minutes, the filed document, the verbatim vote. Students learn to check the frame against the fact.

Pono — Righteousness — Right Relationship

In the Hawaiian tradition, pono means to be in right relationship — with each other, with the land, with the truth. Civic education done with pono asks not just what the law says, but whether the outcome is just.

Sample Activity
Map Our Community Leaders

Open the Civic Dashboard together. Find your county on the map. Name one official and one job they do. Draw or write: "Our mayor/councilmember is ____. Their job is ____." Post the class results on the wall.

Time: 20 minutes  ·  Materials: Civic Dashboard on projector, paper and crayons  ·  Learning: Community helpers, names of government roles, local place-names

Pono for this level — Government is People Serving People.

The first lesson of civic pono: public servants are our neighbors. They have names, they have jobs, and their decisions are public. This builds the foundation that government is accountable — not distant or abstract — while honoring the Hawaiian value that every person in the community has kuleana (responsibility).
