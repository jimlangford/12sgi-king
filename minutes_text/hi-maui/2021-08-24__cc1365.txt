is to urge NOAA and National Marine Fisheries Service to finalize and publish a
regulation that would prohibit the approach of spinner dolphins in Hawaii within
50 yards for persons, vessels, and objects.
The proposed regulation was initially introduced by NOAA and NMFS in a draft
environmental impact statement in 2016, with a series of public meetings held
throughout the State. After the public comment period on December 2016, it has taken
nearly five years to develop the Final Environmental Impact Statement. The FEIS
highlights five alternatives for potential protection of spinner dolphins.
NOAA/NMFS is recommending that Alternative 3{A) be approved. Alternative 3(A)
would prohibit swimming and approaching Hawaiian spinner dolphins within 50 yards.
And the prohibition applies to all forms of swimming-with and approach in water and
And
air.
There are numerous studies that suggest that close human interaction with dolphins
will change behavioral patterns over time, can lead to habitat displacement, impact
reproductive fitness and population declines, and alter foraging patterns which can
impact their health. The Pacific Whale Foundation Research Program studied spinner
dolphins in Maui Nui and found that human activity such as swimming-with and viewing
wild dolphins from vessels is altering their natural behavior patterns and preventing
them from having adequate rest. We have available today. Stephanie Stack, who led
the research team here, as a resource, should the Council require additional
information.
I also wish to note that on July 21, 2021, the County of Hawaii adopted a similar
resolution urging NOAA to promptly finalize and issue its publication of the proposed
rule protecting spinner dolphins in Hawaii. Even though NOAA/NMFS is moving
forward with the FEIS, there is no clear guidelines when the regulation will take effect.
Therefore, we're asking NOAA/NMFS to move swiftly once public comment period for
the FEIS has concluded to prevent further disruption, disturbance, and harassment of
the spinner dolphins in Hawaii.

Testimony in support of proposed County bill
As a nonprofit working to protect the coral reefs, native fish and ocean water quality in the nearshore
waters of Maui County, Maui Nui Marine Resource Council is in strong support of Bill No. 72, "A BILL
FOR AN ORDINANCE ESTABLISHING CHAPTER 2.50, MAUI COUNTY CODE,
RELATING TO PESTICIDE AND FERTILIZER USE ON COUNTY PROPERTY"
Scientific publications cite the impacts of herbicides, pesticides and synthetic fertilizers on coral reefs
and aquatic life. For example, the report. Chemical Pollution on Coral Reefs: Exposure and Ecological
Effects, published in the Ecological Impacts of Toxic Chemicals notes that "herbicides readily penetrate
coral tissues and rapidly (within minutes) reduce the photosynthetic efficiency of the endosymbiotic
zooxanthellae." Zooxanthelle are the tiny plant ceils that live within the coral polyps and help the coral
survive by providing it with food resulting from photosynthesis. When corals produce less food for
themselves, they become weaker and are more likely to die.
Synthetic petroleum-based fertilizers have been implicated in the nutrification of coral reefs - nuisance
algal growth that causes negative impacts on the reefs. Water soluble fertilizer dissolves and releases
nitrogen rapidly when in contact with soil, moisture, rain and irrigation. This fast-disolving nitrogen only
feeds the plant for a short while. Dissolved nitrogen quickly enters groundwater and is transported into
the ocean. In the ocean, the nitrogen causes excessive algae growth that smothers corals and causes
reef loss.
That's why turf chemicals used at the Waiehu Golf Course are a concern. The reefs of Waiehu are
suffering. To help protect the reefs, we need to remove the stressors of synthetic turf chemicals and
herbicides that are currently transported into the ocean from the golf course.
As ocean water temperatures increase, causing increased coral bleaching, we need to do all we can to
reduce the stressors on our coral reefs. We need our reefs to protect Maui's shorelines and our coastal
infrastructure from erosion, especially in the face of sea level rise and storm surges. Let's not wait. Let's
take action now to protect the reefs of Waiehu and other parts of Maui from unnecessary stress. Please
pass Bill No. 72 - and also please consider accelerating the transition period for Waiehu Golf Course as
outlined in Bill No. 72 to protect our reefs sooner, rather than later - because 3 years from now may be
too late.
Thank you.

The Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA) SUPPORTS the bill proposed in Committee
Report (CR) 21-62, which would provide much-needed tax protections for an additional
category of ancestral lands acquired under the provisions of the Kuleana Act of 1850, and
held within the same ‘ohana since their original acquisition pursuant to the Act. Applying
the county's Kuleana Property Tax Exemption to "government grants" purchased under the
Act would be consistent with and further the intent of both the Act as well as that of the
Exemption, to enable Native Hawaiian and other ‘ohana to maintain ownership of and
connection with their ancestral lands, and thereby avoid exacerbating the unique and
deep harms resulting from the historical and ongoing severance of Native Hawaiians and
others from the ‘aina.
As a preliminary matter, OHA emphasizes that the Westernization of land tenure
in Hawai‘i, and the distribution of land and power that occurred subsequent to the
illegal overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom, have had profound and unique impacts on
the Native Hawaiian people. Such impacts include the physical, spiritual, and
socieoeconomic harms arising from the dispossession of Native Hawaiians' ancestral
lands, and from the myriad other circumstances that have strained or eroded away Native
Hawaiians' connection to the ‘aina that have sustained their ‘ohana since time
immemorial.'' Sadly, much of the lands that Native Hawaiians were able to acquire
during Hawai‘i's transition to a Western property system have already been lost,
abandoned, sold, seized, or stolen, and various factors today continue to place great strain
on the ability of Native Hawaiian families to continue maintaining a connection to, and
ownership of, their ancestral ‘aina.

critical relief to the few remaining Native Hawaiian and other families who have
maintained their family lands since their acquisition under the Kuleana Act, and whose
ability to continue holding onto their lands may be placed under ever increasing strain
due to exorbitant, speculation-driven tax increases. The Kuleana Act of 1850, as

authority to enforce only for Federal and State pesticide laws and rules, under Chapter
149A, HRS, and the Hawaii Administrative Rules, Chapter 4-66. The Department is
granted this authority to enforce federal pesticide laws by FIFRA and through our
cooperative agreement with EPA. Maui County does not have that same authority and
would be unable to enforce State and Federal pesticide laws. The conflicts or
inconsistencies created by this measure jeopardize the cooperative agreement the
Department has with EPA and could potentially impact the Department’s statewide
