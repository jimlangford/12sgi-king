Wā 3 + Wā 5
Start Here — Education Pathway

What is SAGE, what are Wā, and why these two eras matter in the 54-node ahupuaʻa game.

In plain words: SAGE is a strategy game set in Maui. You manage one ahupuaʻa — the traditional Hawaiian land division that runs from the uplands (Mauka) down through the farming belt (Kula) to the sea (Makai). Every node you can plant, fish, or tend comes from a 54-card deck. Each card belongs to a Wā — an era from the Kumulipo, the Hawaiian cosmogonic chant. Wā 3 and Wā 5 are the two eras you'll encounter most on the education pathway: Wā 3 sits in the ocean zone, Wā 5 in the growing fields. Understanding these two eras gives you the conceptual foundation for the whole game.

What is SAGE?

SAGE (Stewardship, Agriculture, Governance, Ecology) is a 54-node card-and-board game built on the ahupuaʻa model — the traditional Hawaiian land management system where mountain, farmland, and ocean are one continuous living system. Each of the 54 nodes represents a real stewardship role: wildfire resilience, fishpond restoration, food sovereignty, shoreline management, and more.

The game runs on a 13-moon cycle drawn from the kaulana mahina (Hawaiian moon calendar). Players make choices during the day — planting, tending, testifying — and the balance of those choices shapes the next cycle. This is the Ao · Pō rhythm: Ao (day) is where choices are made; Pō (night) is where the system balances what the day created.

The 54-node structure at a glance

54 nodes · 4 zones (Mauka, Kula, Makai, Universal) · 4 akua (presiding source-energies) · 16 Wā (eras drawn from the Kumulipo) · 13-moon annual cycle · one ahupuaʻa per player.

Nodes are grouped by zone and Wā. Zone tells you where in the ahupuaʻa the node lives. Wā tells you which era's energy and lineage the node carries.

The Ao · Pō Cycle

The game's rhythm mirrors the sun-to-moon cycle that governs both civic action and the creative system. There are two phases every card can occupy:

Both Wā 3 and Wā 5 are Pō-phase wā. Their nodes speak in the balancing key — the corrective, restorative voice that answers what the day's choices set in motion. In game terms: these cards tend to restore, rebuild, or sustain rather than initiate or expand.

What is a Wā?

In the SAGE game canon, each card carries a kumulipo_wa value — a number from 1 to 16 that identifies which era (wā) of the Kumulipo the card's lineage is drawn from. The Kumulipo is the Hawaiian cosmogonic chant; in the game model, the 16 wā provide the "existing source" that each card draws its balance from.

A card's Wā is not an arbitrary category: it encodes which lineage, which presiding source-energy (akua), and which zone of the ahupuaʻa is in play. Players who understand the Wā structure can read the deck more strategically — knowing, for example, that Wā 3 cards speak to ocean restoration and Wā 5 cards speak to soil and cultivation.

Wā in the game — quick reference

Each card records: kumulipo_wa (era number) · wa_phase (Ao or Pō) · akua (presiding source-energy) · zone (spatial position in the ahupuaʻa). These four together tell you what kind of stewardship energy the card carries.

Wā 3 — Ocean Restoration

3

Wā 3

Phase: Pō · Zone: Makai (ocean) · Akua: Kū, Kanaloa · Element: Water / Ocean · 4 nodes

Wā 3 is seated in the Makai zone — the shoreline and ocean boundary of the ahupuaʻa. Its four nodes (N45 – N48) all work with the ocean: traditional fishing practices, ridge-to-reef stewardship, reef restoration structures, and coastal algae cultivation.

The presiding akua for Wā 3 are Kū and Kanaloa. In the game model, Kū governs strength and standing; Kanaloa governs the deep ocean and long-range connection. Together they frame Wā 3 as the wā of ocean sovereignty and coastal resilience.

Because Wā 3 is Pō-phase, its cards are most powerful as restoring plays: rebuilding fish populations, restoring reef ecology, re-establishing traditional harvest practices that the system has lost.

Wā 5 — The Growing Fields

5

Wā 5

Phase: Pō · Zone: Kula (farming belt) · Akua: Lono · Element: Soil / Growth · 5 nodes

Wā 5 is seated in the Kula zone — the middle farming belt that connects the upland forests to the shoreline. Its five nodes (N24 – N28) all work with soil, cultivation, and food systems: loʻi kalo (taro paddies), aquaponics, comparative indigenous agriculture, forest-garden layers, and vertical urban farming.

The presiding akua for Wā 5 is Lono — associated in the game model with cultivation, growth cycles, and the agricultural abundance that sustains the community. Lono governs all five Wā 5 nodes, making this the most cohesive wā in the deck: five nodes, one akua, one zone, one element.

As a Pō-phase wā, Wā 5 cards do their most powerful work as building plays: establishing the food systems, soil health, and cultivation networks that sustain everything else in the ahupuaʻa over the long cycle.

How Wā 3 and Wā 5 Connect

Wā 3 and Wā 5 are distinct eras with distinct zones, distinct akua, and distinct stewardship domains. But they share three things that make them a natural education pairing:

The connection — two Pō eras, one ahupuaʻa

The ahupuaʻa runs from ridge to reef. Wā 5 (Kula · soil) and Wā 3 (Makai · ocean) are two sections of that same continuous system. What happens in the Kula fields — runoff, soil health, cultivation density — directly affects the Makai shoreline. In game terms, a player who builds strong Wā 5 nodes is also preparing the conditions that make Wā 3 nodes more effective and vice versa.

Both wā operate in Pō: they balance, restore, and rebuild. Neither is the initiating move; both are the answering move after Ao choices have created a gap. Together they represent the interior of the ahupuaʻa — the cultivation and shoreline belt — as a single restoration system that runs on the same rhythm.
