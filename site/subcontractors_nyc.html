Who Is Really Paid Behind the Prime?

When the City hires a prime contractor, the money does not stop there — it flows down to the subcontractors who actually do the work. Most of that chain is invisible. New York's Local Law 44 forces one window open: for every City-financed affordable-housing project, the development team must be disclosed — the borrower, the general contractor (the prime), and every subcontractor under them. This maps those chains: which primes route work to the most subs, and which subs surface behind the most different primes — the firms quietly present on team after team, no matter whose name is on the contract.

This is a disclosure-of-who law, not a payments ledger — it names the firms on each team but carries no dollar amounts. So this page maps the relationships (who works behind whom, and how often), not the money itself. Being a prime or a recurring sub is lawful and ordinary — every line is a question to verify against the record, never an accusation. Firm names folded case/suffix variants ("KOENIG IRON WORKS" = "Koenig Iron Works"). 3,848 subcontractor rows were on projects with no disclosed general contractor and are excluded from the chain joins.
